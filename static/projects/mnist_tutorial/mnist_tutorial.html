MNIST Tutorial

Summary

This is an IEEE-style writing sample which I think may be useful to anyone hoping to learn Tensorflow. The paper itself was written in LaTeX, and you can see how I did that here.

While this format might not be the usual for tutorials, I think you will find it incredibly thorough and easy to follow. Go ahead and print it out, and read with a pen in your hand. If you decide you like this kind of programming, the ability to read IEEE papers (and similar) will come in handy.

Why This Was Written

I took a neural networks class in college, and was unfortunately disappointed in how little modern tools/techniques were used in the class. We learned about Hopfield networks, McCulloch-Pitts neurons and the radial basis function, but we never got to solve any real problems or use any tools of the trade. This disappointment lead to a sub-par homework grade for me, and my professor gave me a way out: do something modern, and write a paper about it.

The paper below is what I wrote. The task of classifying handwritten digits is commonly referred to as the "hello world" of machine learning, and isn't an impressive feat in itself. However, the use of convolutional neural networks and Tensorflow 2.0 make this exercise incredibly useful from a learning perspective -- and incredibly brief. Anyone can train a neural network to read handwritten digits with greater than 99% accuracy in fewer than 50 lines of code with these tools.
